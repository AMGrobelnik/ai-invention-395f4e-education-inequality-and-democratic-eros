## Full Summary

This research artifact validates the empirical premises of the State-Dependent Education Trap hypothesis across four democratic backsliders. Key findings: (1) All four case countries show simultaneous educational expansion and V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index decline, confirming the basic co-occurrence. (2) Social protection selectivity is confirmed in all four cases, though the mechanism differs: Turkey started from <10% GDP social spending with clientelistic social assistance; Venezuela used oil-funded targeted misiones confirmed as clientelistic by Penfold-Becerra (2007); Hungary retrenched from 22% to 17% GDP spending while strengthening insider/outsider labor market stratification; Thailand covers health universally but leaves 76% of informal workers without income security. (3) The public sector employment mechanism is directly evidenced by Grzymala-Busse (2007) on post-communist patronage hiring (Hungary in sample), Szikra & Öktem (2023) on civil servant replacement with loyalists in both Hungary and Turkey and loyalty-conditioned public works distribution, and Penfold-Becerra (2007) on clientelistic social fund deployment in Venezuela. Rosenfeld (2021) provides survey evidence of state-dependent autocratic preferences in post-communist countries, but uses an attitudinal DV, a post-communist-only sample, and no welfare universalism control — making it complementary rather than competing. (4) Four counterevidence lines require direct engagement: Acemoglu et al. (2005) null education→democracy effect under FE (the key empirical puzzle); Szikra & Öktem (2023) conclusion that Hungary and Turkey do NOT form a coherent illiberal welfare regime; Paglayan (2022) on indoctrination as an orthogonal alternative mechanism; and Hungary's high pre-retrenchment welfare baseline complicating 'low universalism' framing. (5) No published paper was found explaining the Acemoglu null via welfare-regime-conditioned heterogeneity; no application of the Giesselmann & Schmidt-Catran (2022) DD estimator to education-democracy-welfare interactions exists — both gaps are confirmed real. The DD estimator is the correct tool for the within-unit welfare-conditioned interaction when both education and social protection vary within countries over time. Turkey is the cleanest case fit on all hypothesis dimensions; Venezuela second; Hungary and Thailand are partial fits requiring explicit framing adjustments.
