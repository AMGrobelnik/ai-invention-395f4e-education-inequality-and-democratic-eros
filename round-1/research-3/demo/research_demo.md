# Backslider Welfare Regimes and the Public Sector Employment Channel: Case Evidence

## Summary

This research artifact validates the empirical premises of the State-Dependent Education Trap hypothesis across four democratic backsliders. Key findings: (1) All four case countries show simultaneous educational expansion and V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index decline, confirming the basic co-occurrence. (2) Social protection selectivity is confirmed in all four cases, though the mechanism differs: Turkey started from <10% GDP social spending with clientelistic social assistance; Venezuela used oil-funded targeted misiones confirmed as clientelistic by Penfold-Becerra (2007); Hungary retrenched from 22% to 17% GDP spending while strengthening insider/outsider labor market stratification; Thailand covers health universally but leaves 76% of informal workers without income security. (3) The public sector employment mechanism is directly evidenced by Grzymala-Busse (2007) on post-communist patronage hiring (Hungary in sample), Szikra & Öktem (2023) on civil servant replacement with loyalists in both Hungary and Turkey and loyalty-conditioned public works distribution, and Penfold-Becerra (2007) on clientelistic social fund deployment in Venezuela. Rosenfeld (2021) provides survey evidence of state-dependent autocratic preferences in post-communist countries, but uses an attitudinal DV, a post-communist-only sample, and no welfare universalism control — making it complementary rather than competing. (4) Four counterevidence lines require direct engagement: Acemoglu et al. (2005) null education→democracy effect under FE (the key empirical puzzle); Szikra & Öktem (2023) conclusion that Hungary and Turkey do NOT form a coherent illiberal welfare regime; Paglayan (2022) on indoctrination as an orthogonal alternative mechanism; and Hungary's high pre-retrenchment welfare baseline complicating 'low universalism' framing. (5) No published paper was found explaining the Acemoglu null via welfare-regime-conditioned heterogeneity; no application of the Giesselmann & Schmidt-Catran (2022) DD estimator to education-democracy-welfare interactions exists — both gaps are confirmed real. The DD estimator is the correct tool for the within-unit welfare-conditioned interaction when both education and social protection vary within countries over time. Turkey is the cleanest case fit on all hypothesis dimensions; Venezuela second; Hungary and Thailand are partial fits requiring explicit framing adjustments.

## Research Findings

The State-Dependent Education Trap hypothesis predicts that in low-social-protection-universalism regimes, educational expansion channels graduates into public-sector positions, creating a state-dependent middle class whose material interests align with incumbents, thereby facilitating democratic erosion. This research validates that prediction across four backslider cases and maps the mechanism literature.

**COUNTRY CASE EVIDENCE**

Hungary (Fidesz 2010–present) shows V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index collapse from approximately 0.77 (2007) to roughly 0.32–0.35 by the early 2020s [10]. Social spending fell from nearly 22% of GDP (2010) to 17% (2018) [1]. The retrenchment strengthened insider/outsider stratification: unemployment insurance cut to 3 months (shortest in EU), disability spending sharply reduced, public works distributed with loyalty to the governing party playing a role [1]. Hungary's welfare system shifted toward 'conservative, Bismarckian' selectivity in which 'increased divisions between labour market insiders and outsiders contributed to exclusionary processes in social policy' [1]. Both Hungary and Turkey 'replaced critical high-level civil servants with loyal officials through changing retirement rules' [1]. Grzymala-Busse (2007) places Hungary in her comparative sample with 386 state employees per 1,000 population [6]. Education: mean years of schooling 10.3 (1995) → 11.3 (2010) [11].

Turkey (AKP 2002–present) is the strongest case fit. V-Dem LDem fell to approximately 0.117 by 2024 [10]. Social spending below 10% of GDP at AKP onset (2002), rising to 12% by 2018 — expanding from a very low base [1]. Turkey 'is still in the process of universalizing access to social security' [1]; social assistance 'remained mired in clientelism, compelling compliant behaviour among the beneficiaries' [1]. Tertiary enrollment exploded from 26% (2002) to 79% (2013) [12] — the largest expansion in the case set.

Venezuela (1998–present) shows V-Dem LDem collapsing from 0.617 (1991 peak) to approximately 0.049 (2024) [10]. Chávez-era misiones were not a universal social insurance system but targeted interventions — Penfold-Becerra (2007) provides direct empirical evidence of clientelistic deployment targeting swing electoral areas [7]. University enrollments grew from 670,000 (1998) to 2.5 million (2008) [11], with simultaneous public sector expansion absorbing degree-holders.

Thailand (post-2014 coup) shows a historic V-Dem LDem decline [10]. Upper secondary enrollment 41% (1995) → 81% (2020) [11]. The picture is split: Universal Coverage Scheme in health covers ~76% of the population (partially contradicting 'low universalism'), but income security and pensions remain weak for the ~76% of workers in informal employment, with only ~20% of older persons having old-age protection pre-2008 [9].

**MECHANISM LITERATURE MAP**

On patronage hiring and state capture: Grzymala-Busse (2007) is the foundational comparative account of how post-communist parties converted state employment into political capital [6]. Szikra & Öktem (2023) document the same mechanism operating in real-time in Hungary and Turkey under backsliding [1]. Penfold-Becerra (2007) confirms clientelistic social fund deployment in Venezuela [7].

On selective social protection as channel router: when formal social insurance is limited (Turkey, Venezuela) or retrenching and stratified (Hungary), state employment becomes a premium welfare access point. ILO SDG 1.3.1 coverage data provides cross-national evidence for selectivity [9].

On state-dependent loyalty: Rosenfeld (2021) finds survey evidence that state-dependent workers in 27 post-communist countries show lower democratic demand [2]. Critical limitation: attitudinal DV (not institutional outcomes), post-communist only (excludes Venezuela, Thailand), no welfare universalism control. The proposed paper's ILO administrative data + V-Dem LDem approach is complementary, not competing.

On institutional compliance via tenure security: Helmke (2002) demonstrates that actors with insecure state employment tenure comply strategically with incumbents to preserve their positions [8]. Establishes the micro-logic of the state-dependent loyalty mechanism.

For panel estimation: Giesselmann & Schmidt-Catran (2022) developed the Double Demeaning (DD) estimator for unbiased within-unit interaction estimates in two-way FE panels when both treatment and moderator vary within units over time [3]. No published application to education-democracy-welfare interactions was found.

**COUNTEREVIDENCE THE PAPER MUST ADDRESS**

First, Acemoglu et al. (2005) find no significant education→democracy effect under country FE [4]. The hypothesis predicts this null is an artifact of averaging across welfare regime types with opposing coefficient signs — but this must be demonstrated, not asserted.

Second, Szikra & Öktem (2023) explicitly conclude that 'differences in spending levels and policy content do not suggest that the two cases [Hungary and Turkey] constitute a coherent illiberal welfare state regime' [1]. The paper must reframe the welfare dimension as selectivity/insider-outsider stratification rather than a single low-universalism type.

Third, Paglayan (2022) documents that mass education has been used for curriculum-based indoctrination directly [5] — an orthogonal mechanism. The paper must differentiate empirically, e.g., by showing effects operate through employment channels.

Fourth, Hungary's 22% GDP welfare baseline is not 'low' in absolute terms — the paper must respecify the welfare operationalization as declining selectivity or insider-outsider stratification, not low spending level.

**CONFIRMED GAPS:** No published paper explains the Acemoglu null via welfare-regime-conditioned heterogeneity [4]. No application of the DD estimator [3] to education-democracy-welfare interactions exists. Both gaps are real.
